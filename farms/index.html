Our Farms

From research and development to commercial farming, each of our indoor, vertical farms plays a vital role in the global operation that is HydroFarms. Our growing technology is very modular and can be adapted to different repurposed industrial spaces. As a result, each farm has a history all its own. We have a rich farming history of over 10 years that first started up in the Finger Lakes area of New York. We moved our headquarters to Newark in 2015 to be able to serve the New York metro area. We currently operate the following farms.

Global Headquarters

212 Rome Street, Newark, NJ, USA

 

  • Size: 70,000 sq. ft.
  • Harvest: Up to 2 million pounds per year
  • First Seeding: September 2016

Before 212 Rome was the global headquarters for HydroFarms, it was a former steel mill: over 75 years old and in need of a makeover. Now part of RBH Group’s Maker’s Village project in the historic Ironbound district of Newark, our Global Headquarters is the largest indoor vertical farm in the world based on annual growing capacity.

Research and Development Farm

89 Market Street, Newark, NJ, USA

 

  • Size: 5500 sq. ft.
  • Harvest: R&D
  • First Seeding: November 2013

Our research and development facility was once a nightclub in downtown Newark. Now it is the place where we rethink the fundamentals of agriculture everyday, perfecting our engineering and growing algorithms for optimal taste, texture, color, nutrition, and yield.

VIEW ON MAP

Newark Farm

400 Ferry Street, Newark, NJ, USA

 

  • Size: 30,000 sq. ft.
  • First Seeding: August 2015

This former paintball and laser tag arena boasts 30,000 square feet of growing space and some impressive spray paint work. Opened to meet intense market demand for our greens, it is now a productive working farm that feeds the people of Newark and the surrounding area.

VIEW ON MAP

School Farm

Phillips Academy, Newark, NJ, USA

 

  • Size: 50 sq. ft.
  • First Seeding: September 2011

In the heart of Newark, Phillips Academy Charter School students harvest their own greens from an HydroFarms unit in their Dining Hall. The students gain access to fresh, healthy food that can be hard to find in parts of their city, along with a deep connection to how food is produced and why methods matter.
